Summary: Elementary Symmetric Polynomials for Quartic Roots

Consider the monic quartic polynomial with roots \( \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, \lambda_4 \):

$$ p(\lambda) = \lambda^4 - e_1 \lambda^3 + e_2 \lambda^2 - e_3 \lambda + e_4 = 0 $$

Where the elementary symmetric sums are:

  • \( e_1 = \sum_i \lambda_i \) (sum of roots),
  • \( e_2 = \sum_{i < j} \lambda_i \lambda_j \) (sum of pairwise products),
  • \( e_3 = \sum_{i < j < k} \lambda_i \lambda_j \lambda_k \),
  • \( e_4 = \prod_i \lambda_i \) (product).

Statistical Interpretation: Mean and Standard Deviation

The roots form a natural dataset of size \( N=4 \). The sample mean is:

$$ \mu = \frac{e_1}{4} $$

The sum of squared deviations is \( S_2 = \sum_i (\lambda_i - \mu)^2 = \frac{3}{4} e_1^2 - 2 e_2 \).

The sample variance (unbiased, with \( N-1=3 \) correction) is:

$$ s^2 = \frac{S_2}{3} = \frac{e_1^2}{4} - \frac{2}{3} e_2 $$

And the sample standard deviation factors as:

$$ s = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \sqrt{ \frac{3}{4} e_1^2 - 2 e_2 } $$

The \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N-1}} \) arises from Bessel's correction in sample statistics.

Coulomb-Like Analogy for Pairwise Repulsion

In the random matrix/Coulomb gas view, roots repel like 2D charges with logarithmic potential \( V \propto -\log|\lambda_i - \lambda_j| \), yielding force \( F_{ij} \propto \frac{1}{\lambda_i - \lambda_j} \).

An effective two-body form (treating charges as \( \mu + s \) and \( \mu - s \), separation \( r = 2s \)) gives:

$$ F \propto \frac{|\mu + s| \cdot |\mu - s|}{r} = \frac{\mu^2 - s^2}{2s} $$

Substituting the expressions in \( e_1, e_2 \):

$$ \mu^2 - s^2 = -\frac{3 e_1^2}{16} + \frac{2}{3} e_2 $$

Thus,

$$ F \propto \frac{ \frac{2}{3} e_2 - \frac{3}{16} e_1^2 }{2s} $$

This captures the net repulsion strength from symmetric polynomials, with \( e_2 \) reflecting pairwise interactions.

Notes

  • Higher \( e_3, e_4 \) enter skewness/kurtosis but not mean/variance.
  • For symmetric depressed quartic (roots \( \pm a, \pm b \); \( e_1 = e_3 = 0 \)): \( s = \sqrt{ \frac{2}{3} (a^2 + b^2) } \), \( e_2 = a^2 + b^2 \).
  • This is exact for \( N=4 \); generalizes statistically to Î²-ensembles for larger \( N \).
